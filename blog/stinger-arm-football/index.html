Stinger Arm in Football Injury: Symptoms and PT Burner Treatment

By Dr. Ezra Miller, PT, DPT  ·  2025-06-24  ·  Boca Raton, FL

Highlights

  • A stinger arm injury in football causes sudden burning, numbness, or weakness in one arm—often after a tackle—and signals nerve trauma in the neck or shoulder.

  • While symptoms may fade quickly, recurring stingers or lasting weakness require physical therapy to avoid long-term damage and restore safe athletic function.

  • Recovery should include rest, guided rehabilitation, and clearance before returning to play—rushing back too soon increases the risk of permanent nerve issues.

A stinger arm in football injury can feel like a sharp, electric shock running down your arm after a tough hit. Recognizing early symptoms and understanding how to respond can make a big difference in both short-term performance and long-term health.

This guide will help you understand what this injury is, how it presents, and why working with a physical therapist—like those at Empower Fitness—can be the key to getting back on the field safely and with confidence.

Keep reading to find out!

What Does a Stinger Mean in Football?

A stinger in football means a sudden nerve injury—usually affecting the neck or shoulder—that causes a sharp, burning sensation down one arm. It happens when the brachial plexus, the nerve network that controls movement and feeling in the arm and hand, is stretched or compressed during impact.

📌 While most stingers aren’t serious, repeated episodes or lingering symptoms can point to underlying issues that need professional attention.

What Are the Symptoms of a Stinger?

The symptoms of a stinger usually appear immediately after a hit and can include:

  • A burning or electric shock sensation down one arm.

  • Numbness or tingling in the shoulder, arm, or hand.

  • Sudden arm weakness, especially when lifting or gripping.

  • A dull ache or warmth around the neck and shoulder.

  • Symptoms that last from a few seconds to several minutes.

Some athletes recover within minutes, while others may experience lingering effects or repeated episodes that need further evaluation.

Upper extremity injuries, including to the shoulder and arm, account for nearly 20% of all football-related injuries, particularly among quarterbacks and linemen. - Journal of Athletic Training

Do Stingers Go Away on Their Own?

Yes, most stingers go away on their own within a few minutes to several hours. However, if symptoms last longer than 24 hours or become frequent, it’s important to seek professional care to avoid long-term nerve damage or instability in the neck and shoulder.

How to Get Rid of a Stinger from Football?

To get rid of a stinger from football, the first step is to rest and stop playing immediately. Applying ice to the neck or shoulder can help with inflammation, and gentle range-of-motion exercises may prevent stiffness. 

Still, the most effective way to recover fully and avoid future injuries is through guided burner arm treatment with a physical therapist—targeting neck mobility, posture, and stability.

When Should I See a Doctor?

You should see a doctor or physical therapist if your symptoms last longer than 24 to 48 hours, if you experience repeated burner arm injuries, notice weakness or coordination issues, or if the pain spreads to both arms or down your spine. 

Persistent or severe stingers may indicate spinal conditions, herniated discs, or more serious nerve damage that requires medical evaluation.

How Long Does It Take to Recover from a Stinger in Football?

Recovering from a stinger in football can take anywhere from a few minutes to several weeks, depending on how severe the injury is. 

Minor cases often resolve within hours, while moderate or recurring stingers—especially those involving weakness—may require multiple rehab sessions and close monitoring before returning to play.

Arm injuries in football are most commonly the result of direct trauma during tackling or being tackled. The forearm, wrist, and hand are especially vulnerable due to limited protection. - American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons

Can You Play with a Stinger?

You can technically play with a stinger, but it’s not recommended. Even if the pain fades quickly, the affected nerve may still be vulnerable. Continuing to play without full recovery increases the risk of more severe injuries, including permanent nerve damage or chronic instability.

📌 Coaches, trainers, and athletes should treat even minor stingers as a signal to pause, evaluate, and rehabilitate properly.

Stinger Arm in Football Injury Final Ideas: When Is It Safe to Return to Sports?

It’s safe to return to sports when you feel full strength, complete range of motion, and no numbness or tingling. You should also receive neurological clearance and feel confident in your movements—without compensating or protecting the injured area.

Rushing back too early increases the risk of recurring injuries, each one potentially more severe. With expert physical therapy and a guided return-to-play plan, you can rebuild performance and protect your long-term health.
