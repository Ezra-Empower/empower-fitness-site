13 Ankle Mobility Exercises to Do at Home [Easy Guide]

By Dr. Ezra Miller, PT, DPT  ·  2025-07-02  ·  Boca Raton, FL

Highlights

  • Ankle mobility is essential for proper movement, balance, and injury prevention.

  • Common restrictions include tight calves, joint stiffness, and past injuries.

  • Improved dorsiflexion can boost performance in walking, running, and squatting.

Struggling with stiff ankles or limited mobility when you walk or exercise? You’re not alone. Ankle mobility exercises can help restore full range of motion, reduce pain, and prevent injuries-especially when done consistently.

What Is Ankle Mobility?

Ankle mobility is the ability of the ankle joint to move freely through its full range of motion, especially in dorsiflexion (bringing the toes toward the shin). It plays a key role in movements like walking, running, squatting, and jumping.

Good ankle mobility supports proper alignment, balance, and movement efficiency, while poor mobility can lead to compensations in the knees, hips, or lower back, increasing the risk of injury. It depends not only on the joint but also on the flexibility and strength of surrounding muscles and tendons, such as the calves and Achilles tendon.

Why Do I Have No Mobility in My Ankles?

You may have limited ankle mobility due to tight muscles, old injuries, or habits that restrict your joint’s natural movement. For example, tight calves or a stiff Achilles tendon can reduce your ability to flex the ankle properly.

What Restricts Ankle Dorsiflexion?

The cause of ankle dorsiflexion restriction can be:

  • Tight calves (gastrocnemius and soleus muscles)

  • Stiff Achilles tendon

  • Previous ankle injuries (sprains, fractures)

  • Joint impingement or inflammation

  • Wearing high heels or rigid shoes often

13 Stretches & Exercises To Improve Your Ankle Mobility

Stretches and exercises are fundamental for improving ankle mobility. Here are 13 effective options to try at home:

1. Ankle Circles

Sit or lie down and lift one leg. Rotate your ankle slowly clockwise and counterclockwise. Repeat 10 times in each direction for both feet. This exercise promotes flexibility and helps loosen the joint.

2. Calf Stretch (Wall)

Stand facing a wall, step one foot back, and press the heel down. Hold this position for 30 seconds. Repeat 2–3 times on each side. This stretch targets the calf muscles and can improve dorsiflexion.

3. Heel Raises

Stand with your feet hip-width apart. Slowly raise your heels off the ground and then lower them back down. Aim for 3 sets of 12 repetitions. This exercise strengthens the calves and improves overall ankle stability.

4. Toe Raises

Stand tall and lift your toes while keeping your heels grounded. Repeat for 3 sets of 15 repetitions. This exercise helps strengthen the muscles in the front of the lower leg and enhances balance.

5. Knee-to-Wall Ankle Mobilization

Stand in front of a wall. Bend your front knee to tap the wall without lifting your heel off the ground. Perform 10–12 repetitions on each side. This movement improves ankle flexibility and range of motion.

6. Resistance Band Ankle Flexion

Sit with your legs extended and loop a resistance band around the ball of your foot. Pull your toes toward you against the resistance. Repeat for 15 repetitions. This exercise strengthens the muscles involved in dorsiflexion.

7. Banded Dorsiflexion

Anchor a resistance band in front of your foot. Step into the band and drive your knee forward while keeping your heel down. Perform 10–15 repetitions. This targets both strength and mobility in the ankle.

8. Single-Leg Balance

Stand on one foot for 30 seconds, then switch sides. To increase the challenge, close your eyes or stand on a pillow. This exercise enhances balance and proprioception, which are essential for ankle stability.

9. Foam Roller Calf Release

Sit on the floor and place a foam roller under one calf. Slowly roll from ankle to knee, pausing on tight spots. Repeat for 1–2 minutes per leg. This technique helps reduce muscle tension and promotes relaxation in the calves.

10. Towel Scrunches

Place a towel flat on the floor and sit with your feet on top. Use your toes to scrunch the towel toward you. Complete 3 sets of 10–12 reps. This strengthens the small muscles in your feet that support ankle rehab and balance.

11. Deep Squat Hold

Hold a deep squat position with your heels down for 20–30 seconds. This stretch targets both the ankles and hips, promoting flexibility and mobility in the lower body.

12. Ankle Alphabet

While seated, lift your foot off the ground and draw the alphabet in the air with your big toe. Repeat with both feet. This exercise encourages a full range of motion in the ankle joint.

13. Standing Ankle Mobilization

Shift your weight forward over one foot, keeping your heel down. Hold this position for 10 seconds and repeat 10 times. This movement enhances dorsiflexion and overall ankle mobility.

How Long Does It Take for Ankle Mobility to Improve?

Ankle mobility usually improves within 2 to 4 weeks when you follow a consistent routine of stretching and strengthening exercises. Daily practice helps increase flexibility, enhance dorsiflexion, and support proper joint function.

📌 If you’ve had long-standing mobility issues or a past injury, it may take more time to see lasting results-but progress is possible with the right plan and patience.

FAQs About Ankle Mobility

Should You Do Ankle Mobility Every Day?

Yes, you should do ankle mobility exercises daily, especially if you’re recovering from an injury or dealing with stiffness. Short, consistent sessions are more effective than occasional longer routines and help maintain long-term flexibility and function.

What Does Poor Ankle Mobility Look Like?

Poor ankle mobility often shows up as difficulty squatting, lifting your heels off the ground, or walking with your feet turned out. These movement patterns suggest limited dorsiflexion and may affect your balance and overall body mechanics.

What Are Weak Ankles a Symptom Of?

Weak ankles can be a symptom of past injuries, muscle imbalances, or nerve issues. They may also result from underuse or lack of ankle-strengthening exercises. Identifying the root cause is essential to restore proper function and stability.

Should I Wear an Ankle Brace All the Time?

No, you shouldn’t wear an ankle brace all the time. While it can offer support during rehab or high-risk activities, relying on it too much may weaken your ankle muscles. Combine short-term bracing with mobility and strengthening exercises for better long-term outcomes.

Conclusion: Can Ankle Weakness Be Corrected?

Yes, ankle weakness can be corrected-with consistent ankle mobility exercises, daily stretching, and targeted strengthening routines. These habits restore joint function, improve flexibility, and prevent future injuries over time.
