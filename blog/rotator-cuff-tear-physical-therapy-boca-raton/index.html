Rotator Cuff Tear Physical Therapy in Boca Raton: Conservative Management and Post-Surgical Rehab

By Dr. Ezra Miller, PT, DPT  ·  Empower Fitness  ·  Boca Raton, FL

A rotator cuff tear diagnosis can range from 'minor finding on MRI' to 'significant functional limitation' depending on the tear type, size, and your individual demands. The nuanced truth: many full-thickness rotator cuff tears are managed successfully without surgery — and the research increasingly supports this. I treat rotator cuff conditions throughout Boca Raton, for both conservative management and post-surgical rehabilitation.

Rotator Cuff Anatomy and Tear Types

The rotator cuff is a group of four muscles and their tendons that stabilize the shoulder joint and produce rotation: supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, and subscapularis. The supraspinatus is by far the most commonly torn.

Tear classification:

  • Partial thickness: Less than 50% of tendon thickness. Common in overhead athletes and adults over 40. Very responsive to conservative PT.
  • Full thickness (complete): Through the full tendon thickness. May be small, medium, large, or massive (involving multiple tendons).
  • Acute vs. degenerative: Acute tears from trauma; degenerative tears on a background of long-standing tendinopathy. Degenerative full-thickness tears are common in adults over 60 — many are asymptomatic.

Who Doesn't Need Surgery: The Conservative Case

Guidelines now support conservative PT as first-line management for full-thickness rotator cuff tears in:

  • Adults over 60 without significant strength loss
  • Low-demand patients (recreational activity rather than overhead sport)
  • Chronic degenerative tears with fatty infiltration (reduced surgical benefit)
  • Medical comorbidities that increase surgical risk

Conservative PT for rotator cuff tear focuses on periscapular and rotator cuff strengthening — not the torn tendon per se, but the compensatory musculature that can restore full function despite the structural defect. Many patients with significant tears achieve excellent functional outcomes without surgery.

Post-Surgical Rotator Cuff Rehabilitation

After surgical repair, the rehabilitation protocol is determined by the surgeon and depends on tear size, tissue quality, and repair technique. General principles:

  • Weeks 0–6: Sling immobilization. Passive range of motion only — protecting the repair.
  • Weeks 6–12: Active-assisted and active range of motion. Beginning light strengthening.
  • Weeks 12–20: Progressive strengthening. Scapular stabilization. Advancing toward functional use.
  • Months 5–6+: Return to full activity. Overhead sport clearance typically at 6–9 months.

In-home PT for post-surgical rotator cuff rehab is ideal — frequency in the early months matters enormously, and the barrier of clinic transportation is particularly significant when you're in a sling.

Common Questions

Can a rotator cuff tear heal without surgery?

The tear itself doesn't heal without surgical repair. However, the shoulder can regain full or near-full function through strengthening the surrounding musculature — many patients never need surgery.

How long is rotator cuff PT?

Conservative management: 3–6 months. Post-surgical: 6–9 months for full return to sport or overhead activity.

How do I know if my rotator cuff is torn?

MRI is the gold standard for confirming and characterizing a tear. Clinical assessment (shoulder strength testing, specific provocative tests) can suggest the diagnosis before imaging.
